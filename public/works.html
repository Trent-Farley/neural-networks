How It Works

Introduction

It all starts with the data. The amount of information that the internet is pumping out per day is incredible. Just on one platform, Instagram has 100 million images uploaded per day. Yet, every time someone searches for a photo it usually returns a photo almost instantly. This is taken for granted, but the amount of algorithms and processes going through these photos, videos, gifs, memes, etc is increasing to handle the data.

Sorting information is tough. Just thinking about how to sort 100 million of anything is mind-boggling. This task would be impossible in anyone's lifetime. This is where a framework to sort these items would be beneficial and if not used, would mean never finding the photo that you uploaded a week ago. The quickest way to sort this is to just know where the information is. Much like knowing where to get socks in the morning, computer scientists have made a neural network to know where everything is.

The Technical Details

Neural networks are able to process any information given, as long as the starting algorithm can handle it. For this information, however, I will use images as an example, but anything can be used. Since images are making up more and more of the internet, the inner workings of how these get processed are valuable to everyone. There are three different ways to process information:

  • NYU Object Recognition Benchmark (NORB)
  • Canadian Institute for Advanced Research (CIFAR10)
  • Mixed National Institute of Standards and Technology (MNST).

Much like children, neural networks are not very smart when they start. These networks must be trained, but not how a person would train a dog or teach a child. The network tries to do something and if it fails, that part of the network is no longer in the next generation. Just like a single person is called an individual, a single part of a network is called a node. After taking all of the better nodes, the network is trained repeatedly until it is getting all of the answers correct.

The first type, NORB is completely trained within five generations of neurons. The error rate is around 2.5% which is extraordinary compared to the amount of data it is fed. Unlike humans, computers do not recognize features as we do. For example, when a person sees a cat, this is evident because it has features that place this into our brains. Computers do not work this way, they require hundreds of thousands of photos to recognize that it is a cat.

As shown above, the evolution of a network starts with a base amount of nodes. If the goal is completed, the node gets passed to the next generation. Once the network is trained, it can be used for many years, or until it is no longer fast enough. Each generation could have billions of nodes, but for simplicity, this picture just shows generations of two or three nodes.
